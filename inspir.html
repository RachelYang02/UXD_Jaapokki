Competitor Systems

Good360


https://good360.org - (Rachel)
Good360 connects companies and individuals to nonprofits who need donations. They allow donors to fill out forms detailing what type of donation they want to make, and then they take the steps behind the scenes to find a non-profit that wants to accept the donation.

What Good360 does well is it helps the donors to really connect with the process of donating. They splash all over their front page their statistics of everyone they’ve helped, and they promote that donors will get to see the impact of what they donate. This is important, since it's come up in conversations. For example, when talking with the guest from the Cubed school, he stressed how he was tired of donating to Goodwill because he’d rather know where he was donating to, who was going to be positively impacted.

Good360 also has a good base for what the donation form should look like, including fields for donors to input information like a description, quality (and units of measure), photos, whether the donor can ship straight to the recipient or now, whether the donation can be split between multiple nonprofits, etc. They also provide an option between donating goods and donating cash. We’d probably borrow some elements from this donation process.

What they don’t do well is a clean UI — sometimes their header disappears when you scroll, making it hard to find the About Us page, the How it Works page, the Our Impact page: all important information for users (especially first-time users) to know. They also clutter their pages with a lot of pictures and a lot of words and a lot of elements that make it feel a bit chaotic, especially when you minimize the page to a smaller view size than full screen.



Related Systems


Too Good To Go

https://itunes.apple.com/gb/app/too-good-to-go-fight-food-waste-save-great-food/id1060683933?mt=8 - (Gaby)
Too Good to Go is a mobile app aimed at decreasing food waste that connects restaurants, bakeries, and supermarkets to customers who pre-purchase leftover food. The app features three ways of searching for leftover shares: a map view, a list view (sorted by distance from the user), and a search function, which allows the user to search by zip code, vendor name, or food type. Selecting a vendor entry gives details about the leftover share offered, including the price of the share and how many are available today (each vendor sells a set number of shares on a given day). All monetary transactions are completed in-app; the vendor deactivates your digital share when they give you the goods.

Our interface could utilize a map feature similar to this one, especially if the map results could be dependent on search input. What is most inspiring about this app, though, is the values it emphasizes. The goal of this app is to eliminate waste within a community.



Craigslist

https://boston.craigslist.org/ - (Sean)
Craigslist is relevant because it’s a platform designed to connect unwanted goods and services with people who want them. It has the option to list requests as well as list things you want to sell. However, there are a number of features we want to include that are not part of the fairly rudimentary Craigslist platform. First, we’d like to make a more intelligent connection between offerings and requests; a potential buyer on Craigslist has to both post a request and search through all the current listings. Our application should be able to simplify those processes into one with significantly less energy on the part of the user. The second is illustrated by the image below. Our app should simplify finding goods and services, perhaps with a tagging system instead of a strict many-tiered hierarchy like Craigslist. Our app won’t look like Craigslist, but it does have valuable lessons to be learned.



DonorsChoose

http://donorschoose.org/ - (Jordan)
Donorschoose.org allows for users to search for causes based on location, what they donation will be used for, school, and teachers. In our app, we plan to allow users to search based on different levels of granularity or with different sets of parameters. Their interface for searching is well organized. First, the user searches with a general term, such as city. Then, after loading an initial list of matches, refinement suggestions appear. Each search result has a quick summary of how a donation will help, allowing users to make informed decisions when donating.
