DESIGN REFINEMENT

the synthesis of the modifications, feedback, and ultimate deliberate design decisions that helped shape our automated prototype.


DESIGN AND FUNCTION

Donatr is a web platform for facilitating collaborative donation. From the homepage, users can search whatever is relevant to their donation needs. The user is automatically presented with a list of donation centers relevant to their search, along with basic information about each result. Clicking on a result shows more detailed information about that specific donation center. From here, users have ideally found enough information to decide if they want to use this donation center and how to get there. Furthermore, users can also schedule a pickup from this page, which entails scheduling for a donation center employee or a volunteer to pick up their items for donating. Before scheduling a pickup, however, the user has to login (which they are prompted to do if they are scheduling a pickup, or they can access from any page in the app).

CHANGES BETWEEN PAPER PROTOTYPE AND AUTOMATED PROTOTYPE

TRADE-OFFS + DOWN-SCOPING

In this prototyping and refinement phase, we chose to stay in a relatively low-fidelity mode of operation. For example, we chose to implement only the mobile layout of our website, whereas the website would in reality have a responsive design that works on desktop, tablet, and mobile. We had to sacrificed an actual mobile webpage for speed of prototyping; we just made it based on a browser window shrunk to phone size. We also chose to omit actual images and graphs from the prototype.



We also down-scoped our project given our time constraints. For example, in this phase we simplified our profile interface and its subsidiaries. We also chose not to prototype the login/signup in a robust manner because its features would be much easier to prototype in code than in graphics. As mentioned elsewhere in this document, login and signup flows have been standardized across many apps and websites, so we would not gain much by visually fleshing out such a well-known interaction without being able to think about feedback speed and types. In addition, we did not prototype what happens after a donor confirms a pickup time for their donation — it would have given them some sort of contact information or in-app messaging system that directly connects donor to volunteer / center. We had gotten feedback in the HE that addressed this piece was missing, and if had the time to prototype this interaction, we could have gotten more useful feedback. Continuing into the next phase of this project, we are choosing to only build out the donor-side interaction, and will not be implementing the volunteer-side interaction due to lack of time.



RESPONSES TO PAPER PROTOTYPE FEEDBACK

We received feedback from our original user tests on our paper prototypes that our homepage search function was too complicated, and that a single search bar might be more usable. As a result, we designed our virtual prototype with a single, “omni” search bar. However, we have since received feedback that our single search bar is too simple. The simplicity of a single search bar can lead to users being unsure about how to use it, whereas a complicated form was too restraining and confusing.



We received feedback that users wanted to be able to connect our app with accounts they use regularly, like Facebook and Google. As a result, we integrated third-party login into our designs for the login/signup page of our virtual prototype.



RESPONSES TO TEAM-INTERNAL DELIBERATIONS

We debated on what information to include on the homepage; information about previous donations, scheduled donations, community outreach, etc. We settled on nothing but a bit of text explaining our website and a search bar. We since got feedback that for return users, that’s wasted space.



RESPONSES TO HEURISTIC EVALUATION FEEDBACK

The following is a response to a heuristic evaluation we received, found here.


We received feedback that we need to think critically about error navigation as we build out our login and signup functions, which we did not have time to prototype robustly in this phase. Furthermore, as login and signup flows are typically very standard across websites and apps, we did not feel strongly about fleshing out this familiar interaction for our prototype, since this would be very implied.


We designed our search results to show more information when clicked, but failed to implement a successful indication of that interaction. We need to rethink what information appears on our search result page.


We learned that the testers found our home page confusing; in particular, they did not know why they were on our website or what to do with the search bar. Expanding upon the text on the homepage to explain that the search bar is an “omni-search bar”, and affords any kind of search (donation location, item, cause), could help mitigate this problem.


Our “schedule pickup” flow is both overly complicated and not specific enough, and could stand to be improved. For example, instead of two different pickup modes (volunteer and employee), we might have one mode with a volunteer/employee option. The scheduling calendar also needs to be clarified—are you booking a window of time, or a specific time?—and could be enhanced by offering integration of the user’s Google calendar, for example.


They were surprisingly unconcerned with knowing anything about the volunteers coming to their homes. However, all of the users we interviewed in the previous phase asked about this, so we’re planning on addressing it.



KEY INSIGHTS

We had a very difficult time prototyping our design using Figma. We found the software surprisingly unintuitive to new users, despite its functional similarity to tools like Adobe Illustrator. A great deal of our time in this phase went toward trying to figure out how to make our prototypes and dealing with bugs in Figma’s software. In the future, we might not choose to work with Figma’s software again.

We learned the importance of providing appropriate context to the people who are testing your prototype. If the testers are not fully aware of the context of the app (such as the intended users and use cases) the feedback they provide might not be as relevant as it had the potential to have. Furthermore, explaining that this was a prototype, not a finalized product, might have been useful. Many comments that we received had to do with certain things being not fully fleshed out, yet part of the point of the heuristic evaluation was “not focus on features that are missing, but would clearly be added”.



QUESTIONS AND FUTURE SHORTCOMINGS

In our next phase, we will need to explore the following topics:

-- Inclusivity + safety with regard to user profiles and communication between users

-- Coming up with a framework for very intuitive and robust searching

-- Finding an unobtrusive but reliable way to alert users about pickups
