NEEDS ANALYSIS

a design brief that reflects what we've learned about the problem we're trying to design for, and the approaches we might take to solve it.


PROBLEM

How might we help connect people who want to donate specific goods to nonprofits and other groups that need those goods via a software interface?



WHO IS AFFECTED

Potential donors who are unsure about if, where, and how they can donate certain items, and donation centers that often have to reject goods that aren’t relevant to their mission or cannot be processed at their facilities.



DONORS

When donating, some of our users prioritize getting it done: they need to get rid of things, and they need to do it in a timely manner. Of our four donor personas, two fall into this category; Nomadic Norman is a potentially altruistic college-aged person who needs to get rid of extra stuff when they move. They don’t think it’s right to just throw perfectly good stuff away, but their main concern is getting everything taken care of in the short time period they have. They care, but time and transportation are limited resources for them, so the activation energy to donate has to be very low. Sudden Sam also falls into this category. Sam is someone who experiences a death in the family and suddenly finds themself responsible for an estate. They are looking to deal with this stuff in an efficient manner and not dwell on the process, but want things to go to good homes. Sam prioritizes convenience over all else.



The other subset of our donors prioritizes doing good: they can afford to spend more time on the donation process in order to give things their best new homes. Woke Wanda is a 40-something mom who gets rid of things to keep the family home in order. She cares about the environment and chooses to donate these things in order to reduce waste, and also to help those in need. She donates frequently, and she has a car, so she is able to bring her things to the best fit donation center without distance being a dominant deciding factor. Bored Bernie is a quintessential do-gooder: they’re in their 60s and seeking to give back to the community. They have a fair amount of free time and choose to volunteer frequently. When they find themself with something they don’t need, they want to ensure it goes to someone who can make good use of it, but they might struggle to leverage technology to do so. They would prefer to find donation acceptors nearby, but they’re the kind of person who would take the bus for upwards of 40 minutes to get something to the right place.




DONATION CENTER COORDINATORS

For our Donation Center Coordinators, we have another two subsets of users that both have the same end-goal: to create the best impact in the community. Our first subset of users believes this is achieved by fully integrating and engaging with the community. Community Carl is intensely passionate about his non-profit’s cause, and wants to promote outreach and awareness of this cause in the community by marketing in person, in public settings. He’s a people-person, and prefers to engage with donors and customers face-to-face. Go-Getter Gertrude is just as passionate about her organization’s cause, but she’s much more tech-savvy. She knows exactly what her donation center needs, and she leverages social media, Craigslist, and other web technology to get those things.



Our second subset of users believes that, in order to best create impact in the community, they need to focus mainly on running their business well and efficiently. Business-Savvy Byron is high up in the management chain of his organization, and he’s old-school. He believes that a good business depends heavily on friendly customer service, and he’s less up-to-date with the newer technology. He wants to create return donors by making donating an easier process (and by including more motivation for donors), and he wants more customers so that his organization can create more revenue. Boots-On-The-Ground Bailey is a little bit less motivated than Byron — he is lower on the rung and treats his job as just that, a job. He is a bit more tech-literate than Business-Savvy Byron, but relies more on being told what to do than taking initiative.

REQUIRED INFORMATION

Through our user research, talking with both donors and donation center employees, we have identified what is important to donors and donation centers and what problems they face. In general, donors tend to care about how easy donating is and what sort of impact they can make with their donations. Their main problem is centered around physically delivering goods to donation centers.

Donation centers care about encouraging and motivating people to donate from the community, not just once, but multiple times. They’re passionate about the cause and mission of their organizations, and they want to give people the opportunity to make a big impact in their community. Their main problem is centered around making it clear what it is they need to be donated, as well as making it easier for people to donate repeatedly.

Our solution needs to be quick and easy to use. Donors typically have busy lives and have varying levels of experience with technology, so our solution needs to be intuitive and involve quick interactions. Having a mobile-friendly solution will add to the ease of use of our product, so emphasizing a mobile-first approach in our development process is important. We need a way to return a list of relevant donation centers to a donor. However, the term “relevant” varies from donor to donor. As such, a donor-user needs a way to filter the results by location, accepted items, and causes supported. In order for donors to make informed, but quick, decisions about their donations, all of the just mentioned information, along with any perks received from donating, should be easy to find for each individual donation center.

Similarly, donation center employees are also busy. They want their businesses and organizations to run smoothly so they can better impact people’s lives. To do so, they need to manage the flow of their goods and specifically the influx of specific, desirable items. Mobile-first is important, but we will need to make sure our solution works nicely on the computer in the donation center. First starting up a page for a center needs to be intuitive, and updating that page also needs to be quick, as it might be done by volunteers. Donation center employees should have a simple experience figuring out what information they need to input and how to input it in order to appear properly in donors’ search results and provide an accurate description of their donation center.




IMPACT

People looking to donate will have an easier experience finding what would be most valuable to donate and how to do so; the activation energy required for donating will decrease. Donation centers will be more likely to receive relevant supplies that they can accept by broadcasting their needs through our service.
